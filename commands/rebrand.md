# /rebrand

Plan a comprehensive rebranding initiative — audit your current brand, define the new direction, and create a phased rollout plan across every touchpoint.

## What This Command Does

This command guides you through a structured rebranding process, from auditing your current brand perception through defining your new brand identity and planning the rollout across all customer-facing touchpoints. It covers brand strategy (positioning, values, personality, voice), visual identity direction (logo, color, typography recommendations), verbal identity (messaging framework, tagline options, naming considerations), and a detailed implementation plan that ensures nothing gets missed during the transition. The output is a rebranding roadmap that your team, designers, and agencies can execute against.

## When to Use

- Pivoting your business model (B2C to B2B, horizontal to vertical, etc.)
- Your brand no longer reflects who you are or who you serve
- Merging with or acquiring another company
- Entering a new market where your current brand does not resonate
- Your visual identity looks dated and undermines credibility
- Customer research reveals a gap between brand perception and reality
- Preparing for a major fundraise, IPO, or public market presence
- Outgrowing a name, visual identity, or positioning from your early days

## How It Works

1. **Audits the current brand** — Reviews existing positioning, visual identity, messaging, and how the brand is perceived by customers, employees, and the market
2. **Identifies the gap** — Determines the specific disconnect between where the brand is today and where it needs to be
3. **Defines brand strategy** — Establishes new positioning, mission, values, personality, and voice
4. **Develops verbal identity** — Creates messaging framework, tagline options, and narrative guidelines
5. **Directs visual identity** — Provides creative direction for logo, color palette, typography, and visual system (to brief a designer or agency)
6. **Maps all touchpoints** — Inventories every place the brand appears and plans the update for each
7. **Plans the rollout** — Creates a phased implementation timeline with priorities, dependencies, and owners
8. **Prepares communications** — Drafts internal and external announcements about the rebrand

The command delegates to the **brand-guardian** and **creative-director** agents, combining brand strategy expertise with creative direction.

## Example Usage

**Input:**
```
/rebrand we're pivoting from B2C to B2B and need to update our entire brand identity
```

**Output:**

```
REBRANDING PLAN: B2C → B2B PIVOT
Current state: Consumer-facing brand with playful identity
Target state: Enterprise-ready brand that signals trust,
expertise, and scalability

━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━

PHASE 1: BRAND AUDIT (Weeks 1-2)

Current Brand Assessment:

Visual identity:
- Logo: Rounded, colorful wordmark — reads as consumer app
- Colors: Bright purple (#7C3AED), coral (#F97316), yellow
  accent — vibrant but not enterprise-appropriate
- Typography: Rounded sans-serif (Nunito) — friendly but
  lacks gravitas
- Imagery: Illustrations, emoji-style icons, lifestyle photos
- Overall: The visual system signals "fun consumer product"
  which actively undermines B2B credibility

Verbal identity:
- Tagline: "Make it easy" — generic, consumer-oriented
- Voice: Casual, exclamation-heavy, emoji-filled
- Messaging: Feature-focused, lacks business outcome language
- Website copy: Speaks to individuals, not organizations

Brand perception (based on your inputs):
- Customers: "Love the product, but my boss thinks it's a toy"
- Prospects: "Looks interesting but not sure it's enterprise-ready"
- Employees: "We've outgrown our startup brand"
- Competitors: Positioned as the "lightweight" option in the market

Gap analysis:
- Visual identity signals consumer, not enterprise
- Messaging speaks to individual users, not decision-makers
- No trust signals (security, compliance, scale)
- Brand personality is playful when it needs to be confident

━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━

PHASE 2: BRAND STRATEGY (Weeks 2-4)

Positioning statement:
  For [growing companies] who need [to solve X at scale],
  [Product] is the [category] that [key differentiator].
  Unlike [competitors], we [unique advantage].

Brand values (revised):
  1. Precision — We build with rigor and deliver with accuracy
  2. Partnership — We succeed when our customers succeed
  3. Transparency — We earn trust through openness
  4. Ambition — We tackle hard problems worth solving

Brand personality:
  Old: Playful, casual, fun, approachable
  New: Confident, clear, expert, approachable

  Keep: Approachability and warmth — this is a genuine
  differentiator against stiff enterprise competitors.
  Lose: Exclamation marks, emoji, forced casualness.
  Add: Authority, precision, business sophistication.

Brand voice guidelines:
  - Write with confidence, not excitement
  - Lead with outcomes, not features
  - Use industry language precisely — no buzzword soup
  - Be direct and concise — respect the reader's time
  - Warm but professional — think trusted advisor, not buddy

━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━

PHASE 3: VERBAL IDENTITY (Weeks 3-5)

Tagline options:
  1. "Built for the way teams actually work"
  2. "Scale with confidence"
  3. "Enterprise performance. Startup agility."
  4. "[Product]: Where serious work gets done"

Messaging framework:

  Primary message:
  "[Product] helps growing companies [achieve outcome]
   with the reliability, security, and scale they need —
   without the complexity they don't."

  Supporting messages:
  - For IT/Security: "Enterprise-grade security and
    compliance from day one. SOC 2, SSO, SAML, and
    audit logs built in — not bolted on."
  - For Executives: "Reduce operational cost by X%
    while scaling from 50 to 5,000 users without
    adding infrastructure headcount."
  - For End Users: "The tool you already love, now
    built to grow with your organization."

Naming considerations:
  Evaluate whether the current product name carries too
  much consumer baggage. Options:
  - Keep the name, rebrand everything else (lowest risk)
  - Keep the name, add an enterprise modifier: "[Name]
    for Business" or "[Name] Enterprise"
  - Full rename (highest risk, highest reward — only if
    the current name is a significant liability)

  Recommendation: Keep the name and rebrand everything
  else. Name recognition has value, and a visual/verbal
  refresh can reposition without losing equity.

━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━

PHASE 4: VISUAL IDENTITY DIRECTION (Weeks 4-7)

Creative brief for designer/agency:

Logo direction:
  - Evolve, don't abandon — maintain recognizable elements
    while adding sophistication
  - Move from rounded wordmark to a cleaner geometric
    treatment
  - Consider a brandmark/icon that works at small sizes
    (favicons, app icons, Slack integrations)
  - The logo should work beautifully in monochrome

Color palette direction:
  Primary: Shift from bright purple to a deeper, more
  authoritative shade (think #4F46E5 range)
  Secondary: Replace coral with a professional complement
  (slate, cool gray, or a muted teal)
  Accent: Keep one warm accent for CTAs and highlights
  System: Add a full gray scale for UI and documentation

Typography direction:
  - Replace Nunito with a typeface that conveys clarity
    and professionalism
  - Recommended: Inter, Söhne, or Untitled Sans for UI;
    paired with a serif for long-form content
  - Ensure the type system includes weights for UI,
    marketing, and documentation

Imagery direction:
  - Replace illustrations with product screenshots and
    data visualizations
  - Photography: Real people in work environments (not
    stock photos of people pointing at screens)
  - Abstract: Geometric patterns or subtle gradients for
    backgrounds, not playful illustrations
  - Iconography: Clean, consistent line icons — not emoji
    or filled cartoon-style icons

PHASE 5: TOUCHPOINT INVENTORY & ROLLOUT (Weeks 6-12)

Priority 1 — Rebrand at launch (Week 8):
  □ Website (homepage, key landing pages, pricing)
  □ Product UI (logo, colors, loading screens)
  □ Email templates (transactional + marketing)
  □ Social media profiles (all platforms)
  □ Sales deck and one-pager
  □ App store listings
  □ Email signatures (company-wide)

Priority 2 — Update within 2 weeks of launch:
  □ Help center and documentation
  □ Blog (header, templates, about page)
  □ Customer-facing reports and dashboards
  □ Proposal and contract templates
  □ Onboarding flows and in-app messaging
  □ Webinar and event templates
  □ Partner-facing materials

Priority 3 — Update within 30 days:
  □ Internal tools and dashboards
  □ Recruiting materials and careers page
  □ Office signage and swag
  □ Legal documents and terms of service
  □ Investor materials
  □ Old blog posts (update header/footer, not content)
  □ Third-party listings (G2, Capterra, Crunchbase)

━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━

PHASE 6: COMMUNICATIONS PLAN

Internal announcement (1 week before external):
  - All-hands presentation explaining the why behind the
    rebrand and what it means for the company's direction
  - Brand guidelines document distributed to every team
  - FAQ for customer-facing teams (sales, CS, support)

External announcement:
  - Blog post: "Why we're evolving our brand" — honest,
    forward-looking, focuses on the pivot and what it
    means for customers
  - Email to existing customers: Reassure continuity,
    explain the evolution, highlight what is improving
  - Social media: Coordinated reveal across platforms
  - PR: Pitch the pivot story to relevant trade press
    (not the rebrand itself — the business transformation)

Customer reassurance:
  - Existing features and pricing are unchanged
  - The rebrand reflects investment in enterprise
    capabilities that benefit everyone
  - Direct line to support for any questions or concerns
```

## Important Notes

- **A rebrand is not a new logo.** A logo refresh without strategic foundation is cosmetics, not rebranding. The agent ensures strategy comes before visual execution.
- **Involve stakeholders early.** Rebrands that happen in a design vacuum fail. The plan includes checkpoints for leadership, customer-facing teams, and key customers.
- **Preserve what works.** Not everything needs to change. The agent identifies brand elements worth keeping and evolving, rather than starting from zero.
- **Budget for the long tail.** The visible launch is 20% of the work. Updating every touchpoint, document, and integration takes months. Plan for it.
- **Hire a professional designer.** The creative direction in this plan is meant to brief a designer or agency, not replace them. Brand identity work requires visual craft that goes beyond strategic direction.
- **The transition period is real.** For weeks or months, old and new brand elements will coexist. The phased rollout plan manages this deliberately rather than pretending it will not happen.
- **Measure the impact.** Track brand awareness, perception, and preference metrics before and after the rebrand. Without measurement, you cannot prove the investment was worthwhile.
